01
Our Coaching Framework

The Club’s junior coaching philosophy is set out in our coaching framework Play. Develop. Excel. This framework defines what we stand for, how we coach, and what success looks like when coaching junior athletes.

What success looks like at Ryde

Players who love hockey, keep improving, and come back year after year. When coaching junior athletes this takes priority over the scoreline.

Our core beliefs
Play

Players who love the game train harder, stay longer and develop faster.

Develop

Every player has the right to improve, regardless of ability or experience.

Excel

Excellence means reaching your potential. That looks different for every player.

Remember

Winning is the outcome of a culture that develops players and keeps them engaged. Build that culture and results follow.

02
Key Principles

There are five coaching principles that define how we coach at Ryde. They are not rules to follow — they are the standard we hold ourselves to and what we measure our success against.

  • 1.Development led. Core skills are the foundation of our coaching because structured skill development is how players improve.
  • 2.Equal opportunity. Players develop on the pitch, not on the bench. Every player gets meaningful pitch time and the chance to play across positions.
  • 3.Team based culture. We coach players to support each other, communicate positively, and compete for the team, not just themselves.
  • 4.Good sportsmanship. How players carry themselves is as important as how they play. Coaches role model the behaviour they expect to see.
  • 5.Celebrating success. Success is not just on the scoreboard. Continually acknowledging progress builds the culture that drives improvement.
Applying the principles

In practice this means drilling the fundamentals, providing equal game time, building team culture, leading by example, and celebrating progress when we see it.

03
The Role of Coach

To be a good coach you don’t need to be an expert. You need to be organised, encouraging, and consistent. The club’s coaching program does the heavy lifting — your job is to deliver it in an environment where players feel safe to try, fail, and improve.

Key responsibilities
  • 1.Follow the club’s skills program and focus on the current module
  • 2.Run structured training sessions using the club’s session template
  • 3.Give each player roughly equal game time and positional variety
  • 4.Communicate clearly with players and parents
  • 5.Model the behaviour you expect — respect, effort, sportsmanship
Your first three weeks — checklist
  • Read the Junior Coaching Charter & Playbook
  • Set expectations with players and parents
  • Learn every player’s name
  • Watch at least 3 coaching videos on the club’s YouTube channel
  • Connect with the other coaches in your age group
  • Confirm your training time, ground and game schedule
04
The Thru-2-32 Program

The research is clear — players who build strong technical foundations early develop faster, perform better under pressure, and stay in the sport longer. With the Brisbane 2032 Olympic Games on the horizon, Ryde Hockey Club’s Thru-2-32 program exists to give every Ryde player the best possible foundation for that journey, wherever it takes them in the sport.

The 4Cs model of skill development

The 4Cs — Collect, Carry, Connect, Contest — are the four essential skill areas of hockey that we want every Ryde player to develop. It is a model that our coaches deliver across the course of a season and flexes to different levels of ability as required.

Core skill areas
  • —Collect: Securely gaining possession of the ball while in motion.
  • —Carry: Maintaining control and advancing the ball whilst under pressure.
  • —Connect: Accurate distribution of the ball to teammates.
  • —Contest: Dispossessing opponents to win possession.
Applying the 4Cs

The 4Cs apply to every player at every level — the difference is where each player is starting from and how far along the skill spectrum they are. A beginner works on collecting the ball cleanly. An advanced player works on collecting it under pressure. The skill is the same but the challenge is calibrated.

04.1
Collect
Securely gaining possession of the ball while in motion

The ability to collect the ball cleanly — in motion, under pressure, on both sides of the body — is the foundation of everything that follows. A player who can’t receive reliably can’t carry, connect or contest effectively. This is where development starts.

Three key coaching points
1
Collection out in front of the body — not underneath or to the side. This gives the player strength, vision and options.
2
Collect in motion with feet offset — receive off both the left and right foot to stay mobile and balanced rather than stationary.
3
Receive facing forward where possible — using hook leads or drifting to receive. This promotes forward flow.
Common mistake

Player is stationary to receive the ball, making them easy to mark and slow to play on.

Coaching cue

Keep feet moving. Collect the ball in motion. The ball finds you rather than you waiting for it.

Skill areas

Fore-stick receive — reverse-stick receive — receive across body — receive going forward — overhead receive — hook leads — post & roll — receive to shoot — pre scanning — intercepting

04.2
Carry
Maintaining control and advancing the ball whilst under pressure

Carrying the ball with confidence under pressure is what separates players who create opportunities from those who simply pass and stand. A player who can carry engages defenders, creates overloads, and gives their team time and options.

Three key coaching points
1
Ball carry out in front at 1 o’clock and 11 o’clock — not to the side of the body. This creates the platform to move left, right or straight ahead with speed and vision.
2
Stick-on-ball at all times — especially when shifting between 1 and 11 o’clock. Losing touch gives the defender the opportunity to tackle and limits passing options.
3
Engage the defender to create a 2v1 — using the 3-metre rule. Get close enough to commit the defender, but not too close. Then make the decision to carry or pass to eliminate.
Common mistake

Player carries the ball on the side or too close to the body, restricting vision and options.

Coaching cue

Ball out in front in a strong carry position. Knees bent and back straight with options left and right.

Skill areas

1 o’clock carry — 11 o’clock carry — left to right drag — right to left drag — left-handed carry — 3D skills — V drag — 1v1 eliminations — 2v1 eliminations — 3v2 eliminations — stick and body feints

04.3
Connect
Accurate distribution of the ball to orchestrate team movements

Connecting with support players — passing accurately and at the right moment — is what turns individual skill into team performance. A player who can connect keeps the ball moving, creates overloads, and relieves pressure. It’s the skill that makes the whole team better.

Three key coaching points
1
Carry the ball in a position to maintain vision — 1 o’clock and 11 o’clock. A player whose head is down can’t see the pass before they need to make it.
2
Stay down on the ball to execute the pass with accuracy. Not making square contact or coming up out of the pass too early is the most common cause of poor distribution.
3
Follow through with the body to generate power and direction. If the left shoulder is pointed in the direction of the pass, the ball is more likely to go there.
Common mistake

Player attempts to pass with the ball too close or too far from their body and fails to make a flat and accurate pass.

Coaching cue

Square contact with the ball is essential for accurate hit, push or slap passes. This requires the right grip and the ball out in front of the left foot.

Skill areas

Stationary and mobile hit — push pass — slap pass — passing with disguise — overhead passes — wall pass — reverse edge pass — tomahawk — goal shooting — deflections — pre scanning

04.4
Contest
Dispossessing opponents to protect the goal and initiate counter attacks

Contesting for the ball isn’t just about tackling. It’s about positioning, timing, and applying pressure that forces errors. A team that contests well wins the ball back quickly, frustrates the opposition, and creates attacking opportunities in the transitions between defence and attack.

Three key coaching points
1
Engage the ball carrier on a 45-degree line. This controls where they can go and channels them toward the fore-stick, the sideline or a supporting defender.
2
Apply stick pressure (poke) to keep the ball carrier’s head down. A player whose head is down can’t see their support players or make good decisions.
3
Mark in-front in-touch (IFIT) — position between the ball and the player you’re marking to create intercept opportunities and deny easy passes.
Common mistake

Player approaches the ball carrier front on with feet square, inviting the ball carrier to move either side.

Coaching cue

Approach on a 45 degree angle with body turned and feet offset. Channel first, then tackle when the time is right. Stay in the contest.

Skill areas

Flat stick tackle — poke tackle — reverse stick tackle — channelling — reverse pressure — shave tackle — front marking (IFIT) — marking from behind — intercepting — defending in the circle — counter control

05
Terminology
Key terms every Ryde coach uses
One club, one language

When every coach at Ryde uses the same terms, players build a shared vocabulary that makes them easier to coach, easier to move between teams, and faster to develop.

Playing zones
Attacking third
Opposition’s 23m line to their goal. Where we score.
Defensive third
Our 23m line to our goal. Protect above everything else.
Midfield
Between the two 23m lines. Where possession is won and lost.
T Spot
Where the circle meets the baseline. Key circle entry point.
Wide T
Where the 23m line meets the sideline. Key attacking position.
Penalty corner
A set piece awarded for a foul inside the circle. PCA = attack, PCD = defence.
Tactical
Outletting
Moving the ball out of defence into attack. A structured pattern.
Pressing
Coordinated team pressure on the ball to win it back quickly.
Counter attack
Attack immediately after winning possession. Fast and direct.
Counter control
Win possession, slow it down. Reset shape rather than attack.
Ball side
The side of the pitch where the ball is. Congested.
Help side
The opposite side of the pitch from where the ball is. Space.
Transfer
Switching the ball from one side of the pitch to the other.
3G
Get — Give — Go. Receive, pass, move. The core possession principle.
Formations
4-3-3
Four defenders, three midfielders, three strikers. Balanced. Suits most age groups.
3-4-3
Three defenders, four midfielders, three strikers. More midfield presence.
06
Playing Positions
Roles and terminology we use
4-3-3 basic structure
Goal Keeper (GK)
Last line of defence. Commands the circle and saves the savables.
Central Defenders (CD)
Anchors the defence and outlets from the back. One is a marker, the other is the ‘free defender’.
Wide Defenders (WD)
Marks opposing strikers and supports outletting. May overlap into midfield when attacking down their side.
Defensive Midfield (DM)
Controls the middle of the pitch. Key distributor in attack. Manages the defensive press.
Attacking Midfield (AM)
Links defence and attack. Marks the opposing AM. Must play higher than the DMs.
Striker (STK)
Positioned high in the attacking half. Responsible for outcomes in the circle and sets the tone in the defensive press.
Spacings between the lines

Defensive, midfield and striker lines must be spaced appropriately to provide layers in attack and defence. If lines become too flat it becomes more difficult to progress the ball and cover opposition counter attacks.

07
Tactical Principles

These principles apply at every age group and every level. They give players and coaches a shared framework for how we want to play the game.

With the ballMake the pitch as big as possible
  • —Stretch vertically and laterally. Forwards push high, defenders hold their line, wide players hug the sidelines. Width and depth make the opposition’s press harder to execute.
  • —Maintain spacings between the lines. Defensive, midfield and striker lines must stay spread to give us layers. Flat teams are easy to press and hard to play through.
  • —Play to the space, not the congestion. Move the ball to where the space is. If it’s congested on the ball side, transfer to the help side.
Without the ballMake the pitch as small as possible
  • —Defend as a unit. The whole team compresses together. A press only works if everyone commits — one player out of position breaks the trap.
  • —Force the ball into pockets. Channel the ball carrier toward the sideline or a congested area to isolate them and get numbers to the ball.
  • —Deny, pressure, intercept. Once trapped, close down their options. Mark in front to deny easy passes, apply pressure to force errors, and look for intercepts to spark the counter.
TransitionsThe moments that decide games
  • —Win possession — counter attack fast. Strikers rush forward immediately to be an option ahead of the ball. Play direct and exploit the opposition before they recover their shape.
  • —Lose possession — retreat and delay. Get goal-side immediately. Don’t chase the ball — delay the opposition, force them wide, and give the team time to recover shape and protect the goalkeeper.
Keep it simple

With younger or less experienced players, start with the concept not the detail. ‘Spread out’ and ‘squeeze together’ are enough. The language becomes more precise as the understanding develops. As players become more experienced, you can move into anticipation, decision-making under pressure, and reading the game two moves ahead. The best tactic is the one your players can actually execute — start with what they can do and build complexity as their skills and understanding grow.

Managing Your Team
Managing rotations

Use a simple substitution order and rotate every 4–5 minutes. Every player should get roughly equal time. Posting the order of subs in the dugout enables players to self-manage rotations.

Game day scenarios
Situation

Winning heavily

Response

Rotate more frequently. Give less experienced players more time in key positions. Set a team challenge like ‘how many passes before we shoot’. Take the focus off the score.

Situation

Losing heavily

Response

Regroup calmly. Pick one simple goal to focus on — such as limiting circle entries. Praise effort and celebrate the small wins. Keep rotations equal.

Situation

A player is upset or disengaged

Response

Get them off the pitch briefly if needed. Speak privately and calmly. Focus on effort. Involve them in the next sub. Tell them what you need from them specifically.

Situation

Managing parents

Response

Set expectations at the first training session about game time, positions, and approach. If a parent is difficult, discuss it after the game and escalate if necessary.

Top Tips
What we know works for coaches
1
Start and finish on time
Families plan around hockey. Respect their time and they’ll do the same.
2
Use players’ names
Every time. It signals that you see them as individuals, not just numbers.
3
Focus on a small number of things
Pick the most important messages and repeat them until they land.
4
Let the game do the teaching
Designer games are a powerful development tool. Make time for them.
5
Celebrate the small stuff
A first clean receive. A good lead. Call it out loudly. It builds culture.
6
Don’t over-coach during the game
Pick one or two things to focus on. Save the detail for training.
7
Ask questions, don’t just give answers
Questioning is more powerful than telling. It builds thinking players.
8
Run a warm up every session
It sets the tone and signals to players that training has started.
9
Talk to the other coaches
The best ideas in this club come from coaches sharing what works.
10
You are the chief energy officer
Your energy is contagious. If you’re having fun, they’re having fun.
